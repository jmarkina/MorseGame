The Code

Morse code was invented in the 19th century. Its beginnings were humble -- an electrical impulse sent down a wire energized an electromagnet which pulled a stylus onto a sheet of paper, leaving either a short mark, a long mark, or no mark at all.

Morse is a cipher. The letters a-z and numbers 0-9 are encoded in strings of dots ("dit") and dashes ("dah").

A short pause separates individual characters (three time units). A long pause separates words (seven time units).

Morse does not encode punctuation or capitalization.

(A time unit is the length of a dot; dashes are three time units long.)

Why Morse?

The first Morse code was transmitted via electrical impulses transmitted over copper wire. However, Morse can be sent with any patterns of long signals, short signals, and pauses.

Flashes of light. Car horns. Blinks. Anything.
